# Environmental Policy Uncertainty

Himadri Palikhe, Georg Schaur, Charles Sims

Abstract: We construct a newspaper-based measure of environmental policy uncertainty in which increases in the number of articles that mention environment-related keywords and the words “uncertain” and “uncertainty” represent an increase in environmental policy uncertainty. To validate our measure, we provide evidence that it captures major environmental policy events and that an increase in environmental policy uncertainty lowers firm-level investment in industries sensitive to environmental policies. Therefore, we provide a new measure of environmental policy uncertainty that predicts economic indicators consistent with existing theory, can be continuously monitored to inform policymakers and industry leaders of the consequences of the policymaking process, and can be applied in empirical work to examine and account for environmental policy uncertainty.

To examine and monitor the consequences of environmental policy uncertainty, the main empirical challenge is measurement, that is, to quantify uncertainty due to policymaking, including changes in regulation, election outcomes, and enforcement, in a practical measure that can be easily monitored and used in standard empirical applications. In this study, we construct a newspaper-based measure of environmental policy uncertainty and provide descriptive as well as macrolevel and firm-level evidence to establish its validity.

We use text analysis of over 300 million US newspaper articles to create a monthly environmental policy uncertainty index for 1985 to 2019. With this measure in hand, we fit a vector autoregressive (VAR) model to US data to provide evidence for the macroeconomic implications of environmental policy uncertainty. This approach subsumes all channels by which uncertainty affects our dependent variable of interest but cannot identify a causal relationship. In addition, we provide firm-level evidence for the effect of environmental policy uncertainty on investment, employment, and stock price volatility of firms active in industries affected by environmental policies.

The VAR approach shows that a positive shock in environmental policy uncertainty foreshadows a decline in investment and employment for the US economy as a whole. Our firm-level evidence shows that as environmental policy uncertainty increases, firms most affected by these policies see a decrease in their investment rates. Our descriptive evidence shows that our uncertainty measure is associated with events such as major policy proposals, policy actions, and elections over time. In summary, we provide a newspaper-based measure of environmental policy uncertainty that predicts standard economic indicators consistent with existing theory, is straightforward to measure and track to inform policymakers and industry leaders, and can be easily applied in empirical applications.

Combined, our measure of environmental policy uncertainty and the empirical results make several contributions to the literature. Baker et al. (2016) are the first to construct newspaper-based measures of policy uncertainty. Based on keywords, they search for newspaper articles that are associated with policy uncertainty. They then generate an index of policy uncertainty based on article counts. They compare their uncertainty index to existing measures of uncertainty and validate their approach by having a large number of people read newspaper articles. They examine the effects of overall economic policy uncertainty and sector-level uncertainties (health, defense, finance, antitrust, etc.) with firm-level regression and a VAR approach. We provide a novel newspaper-based measure of uncertainty specific to environmental policy. In addition, we provide the first evidence of the effects of this measure of environmental policy uncertainty on the macroeconomy and firms in industries that are most sensitive to environmental policy. Second the effect of policy uncertainty may vary by firm size (Kang et al. 2014). We find that as environmental policy uncertainty increases, small environmental firms see a larger increase in firm-level volatility (proxied by stock price volatility) than large firms.

Third, Republican leadership often seeks to oppose or relax environmental policy that is detrimental to economic activity, including investment (McCright et al. 2014; Kim and Urpelainen 2017). In contrast, an increase in environmental policy uncertainty has a more detrimental effect on investment during Republican presidencies than during Democratic presidencies. Therefore, uncertainty related to laxer environmental regulations during Republican presidencies is especially harmful perhaps due to the added uncertainty of whether policies that loosen regulation will pass through Congress or survive in a future administration. Finally, we examine whether bipartisan policymaking affects the consequence of environmental policy uncertainty. Our evidence shows that if control of Congress is divided, then environmental policy uncertainty is less detrimental to investment than when both chambers of Congress are controlled by a single party. We have two explanations for this result. First, even if there is uncertainty associated with policies, the potential outcomes associated with the uncertain policy are expected to be less severe when they have to pass a more balanced Congress. Second, when Congress is split, firms may believe it unlikely that uncertain policies will result in any consequential policy action because they lack clear support from both parties. This evidence shows that bipartisan policymaking mitigates the effects of policy uncertainty. Similarly, the evidence shows that health and financial policy uncertainty also exhibit heterogeneous effects from bipartisan policymaking.

Several theoretical and empirical studies have highlighted a negative relationship between investment and uncertainty focusing on specific, large-scale environmental policy changes such as fuel standards, production tax credits, cost-share subsidy programs, tradeable permit programs, renewable portfolio standards and the Clean Air Interstate Rule (Isik 2004; Wossink and Gardebroek 2006; Fabrizio 2012; Eryilmaz and Homans 2016; Markel et al. 2018; Dorsey 2019; Fried et al. 2019). We make several contributions to this literature. First, while the existing literature focuses on uncertainty related to specific policy decisions, our measure captures uncertainty more generally by considering the entire suite of local, state, and federal environmental policies, including variation due to partisan policy disputes, legal challenges, and transitions in political power. Furthermore, compared to the post-policy evaluation considered in the literature, our newspaper-based measure of policy uncertainty captures expectations of future policy that has not yet and may not come to pass. This forward-looking nature of our measure is more in line with theories of investment under uncertainty (Bernanke 1983; Pindyck 1986).<sup>3</sup>

For example, our data show that the newspaper-based measure of environmental policy uncertainty was relatively low when, for example, the uncertainty surrounding the Clean Air Interstate Rule was high. # 1. THE ENVIRONMENTAL POLICY UNCERTAINTY INDEX

We follow Baker et al. (2016) and develop a newspaper-based approach to measure environmental policy uncertainty. Baker et al. (2016) validate their measure using a rigorous audit involving human reading of 12,000 articles from 1900 to 2012 to verify that the machine scanned articles do actually discuss economic policy uncertainty. The index is considered the most influential application of text analysis in the economics literature to date (Gentzkow et al. 2019), and many research projects outside the environmental economics literature take advantage of the high quality of the index (Stock and Watson 2012; Kang et al. 2014; Wang et al. 2014; Gulen and Ion 2016; Shoag and Veuger 2016). The policy uncertainty index is also policy relevant and carried by commercial data providers like Bloomberg, FRED, Haver, and Reuters.

Newspaper based measures have advantages over common alternative measures of uncertainty. Alexopoulos and Cohen (2009) provide evidence that indices based on text analysis of newspapers provide more explanatory power than stock price volatility because newspapers are more representative of the general sentiment in the economy rather than the actions of select stock market participants. Policy uncertainty proxied by forecast disagreement estimated from surveys of CEOs and forecasters may not accurately capture the uncertainty of all economic agents (Diether et al. 2002; Mankiw et al. 2004; Jurado et al. 2015; Scotti 2016) and could simply represent a difference in opinions of forecasters. Noticing the potential disconnect between measures of uncertainty and economic actions, Bachmann et al. (2013) argue that firm-level surveys are preferable to measure the impact of uncertainty as these surveys capture uncertainty felt by actual decision-makers and not outside experts. Handley and Li (2020) measure the frequency of the word "uncertainty" and its variations in company reports

filed with the US Securities and Exchange Commission to construct a firm-specific uncertainty measure. However, detailed surveys or firm reports that focus on environmental policies do not exist. In the absence of direct measures, newspaper-based measures are a good alternative. For example, Hassan et al. (2019) provide evidence that the Baker et al. (2016) measure of policy uncertainty is highly correlated with more direct measures based on firms’ earnings conference-call transcripts.

A newspaper-based measure specific to environmental policy has the advantage that it identifies the source of the uncertainty (environmental policy and regulation) and is clear about which industries and firms should be affected (firms and industries subject to changing environmental policies and regulations). Approaches based on the variability in aggregate economic measures or stock market volatility will capture many different policy areas, masking the relevance of environmental policy. Following the newspaper-based approach of Baker et al. (2016) also allows us to easily compare the relevance of environmental policy uncertainty with other measures of uncertainty related to financial regulation, health, and defense policy uncertainty.

## 1.1. Text Analysis of Newspaper Articles

We use Newsbank news aggregator to obtain the frequency of articles in US newspapers from January 1985 to December 2019 that discuss environmental policy uncertainty. Newsbank covers around 15,000 local, regional, and national US newspapers. Advanced search features group articles by date, source, country, language, and keywords.<sup>4</sup>

Our search criteria capture articles related to uncertainty regarding policymakers, types of environmental policies, time line, and economic consequences. We first identify articles reporting on general policy uncertainty. Baker et al. (2016) identify the keywords {“economic” or “economy”}, {“uncertain” or “uncertainty”}, and one or more of {“Congress,” “deficit,” “Federal Reserve,” “legislation,” “regulation,” or “White House”} as most relevant to identify articles that discuss economic policy uncertainty. This judgment is based on the human readings of 12,000 newspaper articles. As in Baker et al. (2016), an article must have at least one keyword from these three groups to be counted as an article about economic policy uncertainty.

Among all articles reporting on economic policy uncertainty, articles that contain keywords specific to the environment are then used to construct an index of environmental policy uncertainty. To construct an environmental policy uncertainty index, we divide the monthly count of articles containing the general policy uncertainty and environmental policy related keywords by the total number of articles published that month. Let this measure be $X_t$. Then, we scale monthly measures to their mean and compute the environmental policy uncertainty index $EnvPU \equiv (X_t \times 100)/M$ where $M$ is the mean value of $X_t$ between 1985 and 2009. The result is a normalized time series from 1985 to 2019 expressed relative to the 1985–2009 average value.

The environmental policy uncertainty index will mechanically increase when newspapers are talking about passage or relaxation of regulations and policy. Thus increases in the index may represent periods of heightened policy activity but not necessarily uncertainty. To investigate this, we also construct an environmental policy index, EnvP, which is constructed in the same way as EnvPU except that its keyword list does not include {“uncertain” or “uncertainty”}. ## 1.2. Trends in Environmental Policy Uncertainty

Figure 1A illustrates trends in the environmental policy uncertainty index (EnvPU, light green line). An index value of 198 in January 2017 means that the number of environmental policy uncertainty articles in January 2017 is 1.98 times the average frequency of such articles from 1985 to 2009.

Peaks in the index correspond to major environmental policy events. These include the renewal of the Clean Water Act in 1986, the proposed BTU tax in March 1993, and the reversal of tighter air quality standards in 2011. The index also captures events such as the first worldwide celebration of Earth Day in 1990<sup>8</sup> and the BP oil spill in 2010. These events signal policy uncertainty if policy responds to these events. Appropriately, the index does not capture protracted public debates with little potential impact on US policy. # 3. AGGREGATE ACTIVITY AND POLICY UNCERTAINTY

To examine the relevance of environmental policy uncertainty, we fit a multivariate VAR. The VAR analysis describes the dynamic interactions among EPU, EnvPU, and a set of macroeconomic variables. The VAR analysis cannot identify a causal relationship between environmental policy uncertainty and macroeconomic performance because policy uncertainty can respond to current and anticipated future economic conditions. It does indicate whether high periods of environmental policy uncertainty foreshadow worsening macroeconomic conditions.

Let $V_t$ be a vector including EPU, EnvPU, the log of the S&P 500 index, the federal funds rate, log employment, and log industrial production in month $t$. We fit the model

$$V_t = C_1 V_{t-1} + C_2 V_{t-2} + C_3 V_{t-3} + \epsilon_t, \quad (3)$$

where $C_k$ is a six-by-six matrix of coefficients, and $\epsilon_t$ is a vector of errors for each of the variables. Our primary interest is to examine the effect of a positive shock to environmental policy uncertainty on macroeconomic activity. In general, the elements of the error vector are contemporaneously correlated (nonorthogonal); therefore, we cannot introduce a shock to EnvPU without introducing shocks to our other variables. We recover orthogonal shocks of uncertainty on these variables using a Cholesky decomposition. The principle underlying the Cholesky decomposition is that the first variable in the vector $V_t$ immediately impacts all other variables. The second variable may have an immediate impact on all variables except the first. We place EPU first assuming it affects all variables, including EnvPU (its subset) contemporaneously. Investment data are only available at quarterly frequency. Therefore, we estimate the VAR model on investment on quarterly data separately.<sup>13</sup>

But it takes over 18 months for this effect to materialize and the near-term effect (i.e., 0–2 months after the shock) is positive. Compared to the adjustment in employment, the investment response to the shock is consistently negative, appears more quickly, and is statistically significant from zero (fig. 3B). The political party in power conveys information about the type of policy being considered. # 5. CONCLUSIONS

Using a newspaper-based approach to quantify policy uncertainty, we demonstrate that environmental policy uncertainty has increased dramatically over the past 15 years. This more uncertain environment has had impacts on the investment rates of firms in industries subject to environmental regulations. This finding suggests that results from prior studies that focus on uncertainty in specific environmental policies is widespread. Using a VAR analysis, we verify that these firm-level results manifest at the macro level as decreases in investment. We also show that this more uncertain policy environment has raised stock price volatility for smaller firms. The magnitude of these impacts suggests that environmental policy uncertainty is more damaging than defense, health, or financial policy uncertainty.

Our results are especially pertinent given the current political climate. According to a 2020 survey, Americans are more divided on environmental policy than on any other policy issue in the United States, including gun control, military spending, and immigration (Pew Research Center 2020). The same survey also documents that a majority of Americans say that climate change should be a top priority for Congress and the president, marking a 14% increase from four years ago. This overall desire to combat environmental degradation combined with a growing divergence in what is considered the correct course for environmental policy forces firms to navigate tremendous uncertainty.

Bills that are not based on compromise likely have a greater chance of being revoked in the future and are thus subject to greater uncertainty. Therefore, impacts to investment may be an unintended consequence of the uncertainty created when a single party sets the legislative agenda. Our evidence can inform the policymaking process as well as the setting of the policy agenda by majority parties.

Other strategies that could reduce EnvPU (i.e., acting secretively or quickly to pass bills) may also reduce the quality of new policies. In that case, some policy uncertainty is likely an unavoidable cost of a healthy policymaking process, and our results quantify these costs, allowing policy uncertainty to be considered when weighing the benefits and costs of changes to environmental policy.

As such, policy uncertainty may be an unavoidable cost or an unintended consequence of the policymaking process. Testing these hypotheses is beyond the scope of the current study, and we will leave it for future research.

There are several other potentially fruitful avenues for future work. Our results indicate that the size of firms and the political party in power influences how the stock market and firms interpret news about environmental policy. Further attention should be devoted to identifying other sources of heterogeneity such as characteristics of the policies themselves (e.g., taxes vs. subsidies). Further attention should also be devoted to isolating the effect of different media markets when measuring impacts of environmental policy uncertainty. Our work is unable to determine whether the newspaper articles used in our index are conveying information to decision-makers or simply reflecting the information they already have. While this distinction is not critical for our analysis, it will be important for assessing the advantages of other methods for estimating policy uncertainty such as text analysis of a firm's earnings reports.
