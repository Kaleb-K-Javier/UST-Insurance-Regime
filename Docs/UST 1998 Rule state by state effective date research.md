# 1998 UST Upgrade Mandate: Implementation Across 19 States

The federal December 22, 1998 deadline for underground storage tank upgrades was adopted uniformly across all 19 researched states, though significant variations exist in program approval timing, oversight structures, and compliance stringency. **Two states—Ohio and Kentucky (during the 1998 period)—operated under direct federal implementation** rather than state delegation, while the remaining 17 received EPA state program approval, with approval dates ranging from 1990 to 2020.

This report documents the regulatory framework each state used to implement the UST mandate established under 40 CFR Part 280, identifying the primary statutory and administrative code citations, tank cohort definitions, compliance pathways, and enforcement mechanisms.

---

## Federal baseline requirements

The federal UST program established in September 1988 defined **"existing tanks"** as those installed on or before December 22, 1988. These tanks had until December 22, 1998 to meet technical standards through cathodic protection retrofits, internal lining, spill/overfill prevention equipment, or permanent closure. States could receive EPA delegation under 40 CFR Part 281 to administer their own programs "in lieu of" the federal program.

Standard federal exemptions applied across all states: farm/residential tanks **≤1,100 gallons** for noncommercial motor fuel, heating oil tanks for on-premises consumption, septic tanks, pipeline facilities, and tanks **≤110 gallons** capacity.

## States with EPA program approval before December 1998

Seven of the 19 states had formal EPA state program approval in place before the December 22, 1998 deadline, enabling them to administer the upgrade mandate under state authority:

- **Tank Definition**: Installed on or before December 22, 1988 = "existing"
- **Compliance Options**: Cathodic protection, internal lining, combined lining+CP, spill/overfill prevention

## States operating under direct federal implementation

Two states did not have EPA state program approval during the relevant period and operated under direct federal implementation:

### Ohio
- **EPA Approval Status**: NO formal EPA State Program Approval (as of research date)
- **Implementation Type**: Direct federal implementation with state memorandum of agreement
- **EPA Region**: 5
- **Primary Agency**: Ohio Department of Commerce, Division of State Fire Marshal, **Bureau of Underground Storage Tank Regulations (BUSTR)**
- **Secondary Role**: Ohio EPA handles certain corrective action and environmental programs
- **Statute**: Ohio Revised Code Chapter 3737 (Fire Marshal; Fire Safety)
- **Key Sections**: ORC 3737.87 (definitions), ORC 3737.88 (Fire Marshal authority), ORC 3737.881 (installer certification), ORC 3737.90-3737.98 (financial assurance)
- **Administrative Code**: OAC Chapter 1301:7-9 (BUSTR regulations)
- **Key Sections**: OAC 1301:7-9-04 (registration), OAC 1301:7-9-06 (spill/overfill/corrosion), OAC 1301:7-9-07 (release detection), OAC 1301:7-9-18 (delivery prohibition)
- **1998 Deadline**: Federal 40 CFR 280.21 applied directly
- **Registration**: $100 per tank/compartment annually via OTTER database
- **Financial Assurance**: Petroleum Underground Storage Tank Release Compensation Board (PUSTRCB) administers fund

**Ohio's unique split structure** places tank installation, operation, and registration under the State Fire Marshal's BUSTR, while Ohio EPA retains certain environmental authorities. Because Ohio lacks formal state program approval, **both federal 40 CFR Part 280 AND state OAC 1301:7-9 regulations apply simultaneously**—owners must comply with whichever is more stringent.

## Compliance options summary across all states

All 19 states permitted the following upgrade pathways consistent with 40 CFR 280.21:

**Tank Corrosion Protection:**
- Cathodic protection (galvanic/sacrificial anode or impressed current systems)
- Interior lining per API Publication 1631 or NLPA Standard 631
- Combined interior lining plus cathodic protection
- Tank replacement with FRP, composite, or cathodically protected steel

**Spill/Overfill Prevention:**
- Spill catchment basins (minimum 5-gallon capacity)
- Automatic shutoff devices (activate at 95% tank capacity)
- High-level alarms (activate at 90% capacity)
- Ball float valves (restricted in Oklahoma)

**Release Detection:**
- Automatic tank gauging (ATG)
- Statistical inventory reconciliation (SIR)
- Interstitial monitoring (for double-walled systems)
- Groundwater monitoring
- Vapor monitoring
- Monthly inventory control with periodic tightness testing

## Conclusions and key findings

All 19 states adopted the federal December 22, 1998 deadline for existing UST upgrades, with no state extending this date. The tank cohort subject to the mandate was uniformly defined as systems installed on or before December 22, 1988.

**Program approval timing varied significantly.** Seven states (Alabama, Arkansas, Kansas, Louisiana, Maryland, New Mexico, North Dakota) had EPA approval before the 1998 deadline. Eight states received approval between 1998 and 2012 (Tennessee, Virginia, Maine, Massachusetts, Minnesota, North Carolina, Pennsylvania, Idaho). Kentucky received approval only in 2020, and Ohio remains without formal state program approval.

**Ohio and Kentucky (pre-2020) represent notable exceptions** where federal regulations applied directly alongside state regulations, requiring compliance with both frameworks.

**Oklahoma and New Mexico imposed the most stringent overfill requirements**, mandating both 90% alarms AND 95% automatic shutoffs rather than the federal either/or standard. Several states also accelerated secondary containment requirements for new tanks ahead of the federal 2015 deadline.

**Massachusetts and Kentucky established additional phase-out deadlines** beyond December 1998—Massachusetts required removal of all single-walled steel tanks by August 2017, while Kentucky required interior-lined tanks to add cathodic protection or close by December 2013.
